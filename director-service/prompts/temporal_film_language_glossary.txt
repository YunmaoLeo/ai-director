Selected film-language terms adapted for this project from the Columbia Film Language Glossary:

- `aerial shot`: camera observes from high overhead; use for scale, route readability, or tactical overview.
- `crane shot`: camera rises, descends, or sweeps vertically through space; encode with height change across the shot.
- `high-angle shot`: camera looks down from above eye level; useful for surveillance, vulnerability, or geometric clarity.
- `low-angle shot`: camera looks upward from below eye level; useful for dominance, power, or momentum.
- `handheld shot`: camera feels embodied and unstable; use when you want urgency, roughness, or subjectivity.
- `Steadicam shot`: camera glides smoothly through space; use for controlled follow shots with fluid momentum.
- `swish pan`: very fast pan that carries energy and directional force into the next image.
- `dutch angle` / `canted angle`: tilted horizon that introduces instability, menace, or impact.
- `tracking (trucking) shot`: camera moves through space with or alongside the subject rather than only rotating in place.
- `zoom shot`: focal length changes during the shot without requiring major camera translation; express with FOV change.
- `wide-angle lens`: broader field of view and stronger depth exaggeration; useful for speed, scale, or spatial distortion.
- `fisheye lens`: extreme wide-angle look; useful for stylization, pressure, or disorientation.
- `rack focus`: focus plane shifts during the shot; useful for attention transfer or reveal emphasis.
- `shallow focus`: narrow plane of sharpness; use for intimacy, isolation, or emphasis.
- `deep focus`: near and far planes stay legible together; bias toward geography-preserving coverage and wider lens language.
- `point of view (POV)`: subjective view aligned with a character or vehicle.
- `establishing shot`: distant orienting shot that clarifies space before tighter coverage.
- `close-up / medium shot / long shot`: standard scale vocabulary for how much of the subject and surroundings are visible.

When one of these terms is central to the user's intent, keep the term visible in your shot design by using it in `shot_type`, `movement`, or `constraints.film_terms`, and translate it into physical camera controls such as `camera_height`, `camera_height_start`, `camera_height_end`, `fov`, `fov_start`, `fov_end`, `dutch`, `focus_distance`, `aperture`, `focal_length`, `lens_shift`, `lens_profile`, `zoom_profile`, `rig_style`, `vantage`, or `dsl`.
